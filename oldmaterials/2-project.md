# Module 2: Building Your Purpose Statement

## Why Purpose Matters (And Why We're Starting Here)

You know that feeling when you're doing homework at 11 PM and you wonder, "Why am I even doing this?" Or when you're stressed about grades and can't quite remember what you're working toward?

That's what happens without a clear sense of purpose.

Here's what research shows: Students who have a clear sense of purpose—why they're in college, what they're working toward—are more resilient, more engaged, and ultimately more successful. Not because they're smarter. Because they know *why it matters*.

A purpose statement isn't just motivational fluff. It's a compass. When you're overwhelmed, confused, or tempted to quit, it reminds you what you're actually working toward.

Over the next two weeks, we're going to do the deep work of figuring out **your why**—and turn that into a purpose statement that will guide every major decision you make in college.

This might feel uncomfortable. Good. The best things usually do. You're about to get clear on what actually matters to you, not what you think *should* matter. That clarity is gold.

## Part 1: Discovering Your Values

A purpose statement is built on values. So let's start there.

**Values are the principles and qualities that matter most to you.** They're what you care about, what you stand for, what brings you alive.

Your values might be tied to relationships, creativity, learning, helping others, independence, adventure, security, integrity, belonging—the list is endless. Everyone's value set is different. That's the point.

Here's why this matters: When your choices align with your values, life feels meaningful. When they don't, you feel conflicted and drained.

### Exercise: What Matters to You?

Let me walk you through this. Take 15-20 minutes and do this exercise. Seriously. Don't skip it.

**Part A: Reflect on moments when you felt alive**

Think back to a time when you felt energized, proud, or genuinely happy. Not just "fine" or "accomplished." Really alive. 

It could be:
- A moment with friends or family
- Completing a project or challenge
- Helping someone
- Learning something that fascinated you
- Creating something
- Having a meaningful conversation
- Pursuing a passion or hobby

Close your eyes. Actually remember what that felt like.

Now ask yourself:
- What was I doing?
- Who was I with (or was I alone)?
- What values were being honored in that moment? (If you're not sure, I'll help you name them below)
- What did this moment have in common with other moments when I felt alive?

**Part C: Narrow down to your core 5**

You selected 5-8. Now look at that list. If you could only honor 5 values for the rest of your life, which 5 would they be? These are your *core values*—the non-negotiables.

Write them down. Next to each, write one sentence about what it means to you.

Example:
- **Authenticity:** Being real and honest, even when it's uncomfortable. Living according to what I believe, not what others expect.
- **Growth:** Constantly learning and developing. Not staying the same; always pushing myself to get better.
- **Belonging:** Having people I love and who love me. Being part of communities that matter to me.

**This is important. Don't skip it.**

## Part 2: Defining Your Goals

Now that you're clear on your values, let's look at your goals. And I mean *your* goals, not your parents' goals or society's expectations. Your actual goals.

Goals are different from values. Values are who you are and what you stand for. Goals are what you want to accomplish—things you're working toward.

Let's break this into three categories that align with the learning outcomes you're building toward.

### Academic Goals

This is about your education. Not grades (though those might be involved). **What do you actually want to learn and develop?**

Some questions to consider:
- What subjects fascinate you?
- What skills do you want to develop? (Writing? Leadership? Technical skills? Analytical thinking?)
- What degree are you pursuing, and why does it matter to you?
- What would make you feel proud of your academic work?
- How does your education connect to your bigger life goals?

Your academic goal might sound like:
- "I want to develop strong writing skills so I can communicate my ideas clearly in any career"
- "I want to deeply understand how the human mind works and contribute to mental health awareness"
- "I want to build technical skills that prepare me for a career in software development"
- "I want to become a knowledgeable educator who inspires young people"

**What's your academic goal?** Write it down. One or two sentences.

### Career Goals

This is about your future work and professional life. Again, not what you *should* do. What actually excites you?

Some questions to consider:
- What kind of work sounds meaningful to you?
- What would you do even if it didn't pay much (but it does, so dream big)?
- What impact do you want to have through your work?
- What kind of work environment appeals to you? (Helping people? Creating things? Solving problems? Teaching? Entrepreneurial? Structured? Flexible?)
- What does success look like in your career?

Your career goal might sound like:
- "I want to work in healthcare, ideally helping underserved communities access quality mental health care"
- "I want to start my own business that creates sustainable products"
- "I want to be a teacher who makes science come alive for students"
- "I want to work in nonprofit leadership, driving policy changes that matter"

**What's your career goal?** Write it down. One or two sentences. (If you're not sure yet, that's okay. Write what you're curious about or what you're leaning toward.)

### Personal Goals

This is the piece many students skip, and it's often the most important. What kind of person do you want to be? How do you want to live?

Some questions to consider:
- What kind of relationships do you want to have? (Deep friendships? Family closeness? Community?)
- How do you want to spend your time and energy?
- What kind of life sounds good to you? (Fast-paced? Slower? Adventurous? Grounded?)
- What brings you joy outside of work and school?
- What would make you feel like your life is full and meaningful?
- Who do you want to become?

Your personal goal might sound like:
- "I want to build deep, genuine friendships and be someone people can count on"
- "I want to travel and experience different cultures"
- "I want to prioritize my family and be present with people I love"
- "I want to develop a regular creative practice and make things"
- "I want to be healthy and active, and model wellness for my future family"

**What's your personal goal?** Write it down. One or two sentences.

## Part 3: Crafting Your Purpose Statement

Now here's where it comes together. Your purpose statement is where your **values** meet your **goals**. It's the "why" that ties everything together.

A purpose statement answers: *Why are you in college? What are you working toward? How do your choices align with what matters to you?*

It's not a mission statement for a company. It's personal. It's yours.

### What a Good Purpose Statement Looks Like

Here are some examples (these are real, from actual students):

**Example 1:**
"I'm in college to develop the skills and knowledge to become a healthcare advocate. I value helping others and creating systems that work for people who've been overlooked. My education connects to my goal of working in community health. I want to build a career that's meaningful, surrounded by people I love, and that allows me to give back."

**Example 2:**
"I'm pursuing business because I want to understand how organizations work and, eventually, build something of my own. I value independence, innovation, and integrity. My goal is to create products or services that solve real problems. Beyond work, I want to build a life with strong friendships, regular time outdoors, and financial stability."

**Example 3:**
"I'm here because I love learning and want to become a teacher who opens doors for kids who come from backgrounds like mine. I value education, service, and helping others. My goal is to teach in under-resourced schools where my presence can matter. I want a life that's grounded in my community, with family close by, and the knowledge that I'm making a difference."

See how each one is specific, personal, and ties values to goals?

### How to Write Yours

Here's a simple structure. Write 2-4 sentences:

1. **Statement of direction:** What are you studying/pursuing, and why?
2. **Values connection:** What values are guiding your choices?
3. **Goals integration:** How does this connect to your academic, career, and personal goals?
4. **Vision:** What does a life aligned with this purpose look like?

**Template (feel free to use it or ignore it):**

"I'm in college to [academic goal] because [why it matters to you—connect to values]. My goal is to [career goal] in a way that [how your values show up in your work]. Beyond work, I want [personal goal], building a life that [what this looks like—authenticity, connection, balance, impact, etc.]."

### Rules for Your Purpose Statement

- **It's for you, not for impressing anyone**
- **It can change.** You're not writing it in stone. In fact, it probably *will* evolve as you grow.
- **It should feel true.** When you read it, you should feel some sense of "Yeah, that's it."
- **It doesn't have to be eloquent.** Just honest.

## Discussion Prompts & Reflection

Okay, we're going to go deeper with reflection and peer connection.

### Individual Reflection (Do This First)

Spend 20-30 minutes on these questions. Journal, type, voice-record—whatever helps you think.

**1. Your Values in Action**
From the exercise above: What are your core 5 values?

Now: When was the last time you honored each of those values? (When did you actually live them out?)

And: Is there a value you care about but haven't had a chance to express in college yet? What would it look like to honor that value?

**2. Your Goals & Vision**
From the exercise above: What are your academic, career, and personal goals?

Now: How realistic do these feel? Are they things you genuinely want, or things you think you *should* want?

And: What would need to happen for you to move toward these goals? What's one step you could take this month?

**3. Alignment Check**
So far, do you feel your college choices align with your values and goals?

If yes: What evidence do you have? How does it feel?

If no (or "not really"): What's misaligned? What could shift?

**4. Clarity & Confusion**
After doing all this reflection: What feels clear about your purpose? What still feels fuzzy?

Where do you have confidence? Where are you uncertain?

**Take your time. These are hard questions. That's good.**

### Online Discussion: Share & Connect

Post a response to the discussion forum:

**Prompt 1: Core Values**
Share 1-2 of your core values and what they mean to you. How do these values show up in your life right now? Is there one you wish showed up more?

**Prompt 2: Your Goals**
Share one of your goals (academic, career, or personal) and why it matters to you. What excites you about it? What challenges you about pursuing it?

**Prompt 3: Purpose & Uncertainty**
It's okay if you're not totally clear on your purpose yet. Share: What are you clearest about? Where are you still exploring?

Respond to at least two peers. Ask questions. Share what resonates.

## Project Milestone: Draft Your Purpose Statement

This is it. This is the real work.

### What You're Creating

A **personal purpose statement** that articulates:
- Why you're in college (connected to your actual goals, not generic answers)
- What you're working toward (academically, professionally, personally)
- How your values guide your choices
- What success looks like to you

### How to Write It

1. **Gather your work from above:** Your core values, your academic/career/personal goals, and your reflection notes

2. **Draft it:** Use the template I gave you, or write it however feels natural. Aim for 2-4 sentences. (Yes, it can be that short and still be powerful.)

3. **Read it out loud:** Does it feel true? Do you nod when you read it? If something feels off, adjust it.

4. **Share it (if you want to):** Post it in the discussion forum or a private reflective post. (There's an option to make it visible only to me if you want private feedback.)

5. **Refine it:** You'll have time next week to refine based on feedback. This is a draft. It's supposed to evolve.

### Submission Details

- **Format:** Write it in a document (Word, Google Doc, etc.) or type it directly into the submission box
- **Length:** 2-4 sentences
- **Deadline:** End of next week (we have two weeks for this module)
- **What I'll do:** I'll read your statement and offer feedback—what's clear, what might be worth exploring, how it aligns with your goals. I'll be thoughtful and supportive. You'll get revision suggestions if you want them.

### How I'll Give Feedback

I'm going to look for:
- **Clarity:** Do I understand what you're working toward?
- **Authenticity:** Does this feel like *you*, or does it feel like what you think you should say?
- **Integration:** Can I see how your values, goals, and vision fit together?

I'm NOT looking for:
- Perfect grammar (though clarity matters)
- Impressive language
- Proof that you have it all figured out

**Here's what I'll probably say something like:** "I love how you connected X to Y. This is clear. I'm curious about [thing I want to know more about]. Have you thought about...?"

## A Few Things to Remember

**1. This might feel weird at first.** Sitting with big questions about your values and purpose isn't something you're used to. That's normal. Lean into the discomfort.

**2. You don't have to have it all figured out.** This is a starting point, not a final answer. Part of growing is revising your understanding of yourself and what matters.

**3. Your purpose statement is not your identity.** It's a guide. You're not locked in. Things change—you learn, you grow, you discover new things. Your statement can evolve too.

**4. This matters more than you think.** I've seen students come back months later and say, "I re-read my purpose statement when I was considering dropping out, and it reminded me why I'm here." That power? That's real.
