## What This Project Is (And Isn't)

**This isn't:**
- A promise you have to keep forever
- A 5-year plan with no flexibility
- Proof that you have everything figured out
- A formal presentation (unless you want it to be)

**This is:**
- A clear statement of where you're heading *right now*
- What career areas interest you and why
- What lifestyle and values matter to you in work
- How you're building toward these goals
- A communication tool (for yourself, for interviews, for others)

---

## The Core Components

Your career goals presentation should address:

### 1. Your Career Direction(s)

**What career path or field are you considering?**

Be specific, but allow flexibility.

**Examples of specific but flexible:**
- "I'm exploring careers in software development, particularly in fintech or healthcare technology"
- "I'm interested in nonprofit leadership, with a focus on education access and youth development"
- "I want to work in clinical psychology, likely in community mental health or hospital settings"
- "I'm building skills for business leadership, with interest in sustainable business practices"
- "I'm exploring environmental science, particularly in conservation and policy"

Not: "I don't know, I'll figure it out" (okay for now, but time to get more specific)
Not: "Definitely going to be a doctor and nothing else" (maybe, but what if you change your mind?)

### 2. Why This Direction?

Connect your career goals back to your values and interests.

**Why does this matter to you?**

- What problems or fields excite you?
- What kinds of work do you enjoy?
- What values does this career align with?
- How does this connect to your purpose statement?

**Example:**
"I'm interested in nonprofit leadership because I want to build sustainable solutions to education access issues. Growing up with limited educational resources, I deeply value the power of education to change trajectories. I want to be in a position where I can shape organizational vision and impact around educational equity."

### 3. Your Lifestyle and Values

Beyond the job title—what does a good life look like to you?

This includes:
- Work-life balance expectations (How many hours? Travel? Flexibility?)
- Financial goals (What income do you need/want?)
- Location preferences (Where want you live?)
- Type of work environment (Office? Remote? Field work?)
- Autonomy level (How much do you want to decide vs. follow direction?)
- Impact (Individual impact? Organizational impact? Societal?)
- Community (Solo work or team-based?)
- Growth (Continuous learning? Mastery?)

**Example:**
"I want work that's meaningful and allows me to make an impact on education access. I'm willing to work hard, but not at the expense of family time. I have flexibility on location but prefer urban areas with strong educational institutions. I want to work with a team of people committed to equity, and I want continuous learning opportunities where I'm deepening my understanding of nonprofit management and educational systems."

### 4. How You're Building Toward This

Given your remaining time in college:
- What coursework helps build relevant knowledge?
- What internships or experiences will you pursue?
- What skills are you developing?
- What network are you building?

**Example:**
"To move toward nonprofit leadership in education, I'm taking courses in nonprofit management and policy advocacy. I'm planning an internship this summer with a school reform nonprofit to understand operations and community engagement. I'm also building my leadership skills through my role as education chair in [student organization]. I'm reading about nonprofit finance and organizational development, and I'm pursuing informational interviews with education nonprofit leaders to learn about their career paths."

### 5. Openness to Change

Be honest about uncertainty.

- Are there other fields you're exploring?
- What might cause you to adjust your direction?
- What unknowns are you sitting with?
- How will you stay open to learning?

**Example:**
"While I'm pursuing nonprofit leadership in education, I'm also exploring education policy and school administration as potential paths. These all align with my core value of education access. I'm staying open to new information as I deepen my experience. If I discover that direct service resonates more than organizational leadership, I'm willing to adjust. I'm committed to the outcome (education equity) and remaining flexible on the path."

## Format Options for Your Presentation

You can present your career goals in whatever format works for you. Here are some options:

### Option 1: Written Statement

A 2-3 page document that covers all five components above.

*Best for:* Personal clarity, job application materials, portfolio

**Structure:**
- Title: "[Name]'s Career Goals"
- Section 1: My Career Direction (1 paragraph)
- Section 2: Why This Matters to Me (1 paragraph)
- Section 3: My Lifestyle and Values in Work (1-2 paragraphs)
- Section 4: How I'm Building Toward This (1-2 paragraphs)
- Section 5: Openness and Learning (1 paragraph)

### Option 2: Video Message

A 3-5 minute video where you talk through your goals.

*Best for:* Personal and authentic, practice for interviews, showing communication skills

**What to include:**
- Introduce yourself
- Share your career direction and why it matters
- Describe your ideal work environment and lifestyle
- Talk about how you're building toward this
- Share your openness to growth and change

**Production notes:**
- Practice so you're not reading from notes the whole time
- Make eye contact with camera
- Speak clearly
- Keep it natural and genuine

### Option 3: Presentation Deck

A visual presentation (10-15 slides) that walks through your goals.

*Best for:* Practice for interviews, engaging visual learners, portfolio piece

**Slide structure:**
1. Title (your name, "My Career Goals")
2. My Career Direction
3. Why This Matters (personal connection)
4. Values in Work (what lifestyle matters)
5. My Ideal Work Environment
6. How I'm Building Toward This (current actions)
7. Timeline/Milestones
8. Learning & Growth
9. Openness to Evolution
10. Next Steps

### Option 4: Video Interview Format

Record yourself answering key interview questions about your career goals.

*Best for:* Interview preparation, authentic storytelling, demonstrating communication

**Questions to answer:**
- "Tell me about your career goals."
- "Why is this field important to you?"
- "What kind of work environment brings out your best work?"
- "How are you building toward this goal?"
- "What would success look like to you?"

## Key Do's and Don'ts

### Do's

✓ **Be specific enough to be meaningful** (not just "I want a job")
✓ **Connect to your values and purpose** (not just job title)
✓ **Show you're thinking about lifestyle and context** (not just career)
✓ **Be honest about what you don't know** (shows maturity)
✓ **Show you're intentional about building skills** (not waiting for graduation)
✓ **Make it authentic** (your words, your story)

### Don'ts

✗ **Don't make vague promises** ("I'm going to be successful" — what does that mean?)
✗ **Don't present a false version of yourself** (you'll have to maintain it)
✗ **Don't ignore lifestyle and values** (work-life integration matters)
✗ **Don't hide all uncertainty** (everyone has some; transparency builds trust)
✗ **Don't make it someone else's words** (use your own language)
✗ **Don't present it as final and unchangeable** (you're young; flexibility is a strength)

## Using Your Presentation

Once you've created this, you'll use it:

### In Interviews

When interviewing for jobs or internships, you'll get asked: "Where do you see yourself in 5 years?" or "What are your career goals?"

Your presentation gives you a clear answer. You can adapt it based on the opportunity, but you have clarity.

### With Mentors and Advisors

Share your goals with:
- Your academic advisor (they help align courses)
- Your career services advisor (they connect you to opportunities)
- Mentors and professionals (they help you refine direction)
- Professors (they can suggest relevant projects or research)

Their feedback helps you refine and adjust.

### In Your Own Journey

Most importantly: **This is for you.**

When you're frustrated, overwhelmed, or not sure if your efforts matter, you can return to this and remember: "This is why I'm doing this. This is where I'm heading. This makes sense."
