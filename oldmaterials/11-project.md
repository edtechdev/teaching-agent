## What Your Education Plan Includes

An education plan is not just a list of courses. It's a strategic document that shows:

### 1. Your Academic Goal (or Multiple Goals)

What are you working toward academically?

This might be:
- Completing your bachelor's degree with a focus on certain skills
- Preparing for graduate school in a specific field
- Building a specific knowledge base for your career goals
- Getting certified in a particular area
- Preparing for professional licensure (if relevant to your field)

**Example:** "I'm pursuing a Biology degree focused on ecology and conservation. I'm taking courses that deepen my understanding of ecosystem dynamics and preparing for potential graduate school in environmental science."

### 2. Your Major/Minor Requirements

Which courses do you need to take?
- Your major requirements (usually a list provided by your college)
- Your minor requirements (if applicable)
- Which ones you've already taken
- Which ones you still need

### 3. Your General Education / Core Requirements

What other requirements do you need?
- Which ones you've already completed
- Which ones you still need
- When you'll take them

### 4. Your Semester-by-Semester Plan

Here's where it gets real: your actual plan, semester by semester, showing:
- What courses you'll take in each semester
- When you'll take major core vs. electives vs. general education
- How you're balancing difficulty
- When you'll incorporate internships, research, study abroad, or other opportunities

**Format example:**

Semester 1 (Fall [Year]):
- Core: MATH 101 (Calculus)
- Major: BIO 110 (Intro Biology)
- Core: ENG 101 (Writing)
- Elective: PSYCH 101 (Introduction to Psychology)

Semester 2 (Spring [Year]):
[And so on...]

### 5. Special Opportunities

Are you planning to:
- Do an internship? When? Summer? During semester?
- Do research? When would you start?
- Study abroad? When?
- Get a leadership position? Timeline?
- These are course slots too—they happen instead of regular classes.

### 6. Connection to Your Purpose & Career

How does your education plan connect to your purpose statement and career goals?

In a paragraph or two, explain:"My education plan reflects my goal to [purpose]. I'm taking these courses because [why]. This prepares me for [career direction]. By experiences like [internship/research], I'm building [specific skills]."

## Building Your Plan: The Process

### Step 1: Inventory What You've Done

Gather:
- Transcript (what you've already completed)
- Degree audit (what you still need)
- List of requirements in your major

What boxes have you already checked? Mark them.

### Step 2: Identify Hard Requirements

What courses are *required*? Not flexible, not options—required?
- List these
- Note any prerequisites (which have to come first)
- Note if they're offered only certain semesters

These go in your plan first, because you don't have a choice.

### Step 3: Account for Flexibility

From the requirements that have options:
- What are you interested in?
- Which professors are highly recommended?
- Which would best serve your goals?
- Choose these strategically, not randomly

### Step 4: Plan Your Electives

You have flexibility here. Choose based on:
- Your genuine interests and curiosity
- Your career goals (what skills would strengthen them?)
- Balance and time (don't overload)
- What would you regret NOT taking while in college?

### Step 5: Consider Special Opportunities

If you want to do an internship or research:
- When in your timeline does it make sense?
- Usually after sophomore year (you need more knowledge first)
- What semester/summer would work?
- How many course credits would you replace it with?

### Step 6: Build Your Semester Plan

On paper or in a spreadsheet, map out:
- Each semester you have left
- The courses in each semester
- How they balance (not all hard classes, not all easy classes)
- How they sequence (prerequisites before advanced courses)
- Special opportunities if applicable

### Step 7: Check Against Your Purpose

Read your education plan. Then read your purpose statement.

Do they align?
- Are you taking courses that support your purpose?
- Are the choices you're making moving you toward your goals?
- Is this a path you want to walk?

If something feels misaligned, adjust it.

### Step 8: Get Feedback

Take your plan to:
- Your academic advisor (they'll catch any errors, suggest improvements)
- A professor in your major (they know what matters)
- A peer (does it make sense? What would you ask?)

Refine based on feedback.

## Making It Realistic

Your plan should be challenging but achievable.

### Red Flags That Your Plan Is Unrealistic

- You're taking 18+ credits every semester (unless that's normal for your workload)
- You're taking 4+ upper-level courses in one semester (usually too much)
- You're not leaving room for flexibility or reality
- You're overloading next semester to graduate faster
- You're saying yes to every opportunity (internship, research, leadership, clubs) at once

### Green Flags That Your Plan Is Solid

- You're balancing hard and manageable courses
- You're spreading opportunities over your remaining time
- You have electives you're actually excited about
- You're leaving some breathing room
- You can articulate *why* each course is there
- It connects to your purpose and goals

## The Written Plan: Format

Your education plan should be a document you can reference. Here's a structure:

---

## [NAME]'s Education Plan

**Major:** [Your Major]
**Minor:** [If applicable]
**Expected Graduation:** [Date]

### Academic Goal

[1-2 paragraphs on what you're working toward academically and why]

### Degree Requirements Summary

- General Education: [X credits completed, X credits remaining]
- Major Requirements: [X credits completed, X credits remaining]
- Electives/Free Credits: [X credits total available]
- Total Credits Needed: [X]

### Major Courses Completed

[List with semester completed]

### Major Courses Remaining

[List in order of planning]

### Semester-by-Semester Plan

**Fall [Year]: [Total Credits]**
- [Course Code] [Course Name] ([Credits]) - [Why: core/major/elective? Connection to goals?]
- [Course Code] [Course Name] ([Credits])
- [Etc.]

[Continue for each semester]

### Special Opportunities

[Internship, research, study abroad plans if applicable]

### Connection to Purpose & Career

[Paragraph connecting this education plan to your purpose statement and career goals]

---

### Format Notes

Keep it clean and organized. It's for you to reference, so:
- Use a format you like
- Make it easy to read
- Include enough detail to understand each choice
- But don't make it overwhelming

A 2-4 page document is typical.

## Key Takeaways This Week

- **Your education plan is a strategic document.** It shows your intentional path forward.
- **It connects to your purpose and goals.** Not just course checking—it's purposeful.
