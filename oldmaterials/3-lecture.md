# Module 3: College Support Systems & Help-Seeking

## The Power of Asking for Help

Here's something many students don't realize until late in their college career: **Getting help is not a weakness. It's a superpower.**

The students who struggle the most often aren't less intelligent. They're often the ones who tried to do everything alone. They waited until they were drowning before reaching out. Or they never reached out at all.

The students who thrive? Many of them have figured out that every college is built with support systems. And using those resources isn't cheating or admitting failure—it's being smart. It's being strategic. It's understanding that you don't have to figure everything out by yourself.

This week is about demystifying those resources, getting you connected, and building your support network *before* you're in crisis.

Because here's the truth: **You're going to struggle at some point.** A class will feel impossibly hard. Your mental health might dip. You'll question your major. You'll feel overwhelmed. You'll face a personal crisis.

When that happens, you want to know exactly where to go. You want to already have a relationship with people who can help. You want to know that reaching out is normal, easy, and effective.

Let's build that now.

## Understanding College Support Systems

Most colleges have similar types of support. But the names, locations, and how they work can vary. So let's break down the main categories.

### Academic Support

These are resources to help you succeed in your coursework.

**Academic Advising**
- **What it is:** Guidance on course selection, major/minor changes, degree requirements, academic planning
- **Who uses it:** Everyone should, regularly
- **When to go:** Start of each semester for planning; when considering major changes; when struggling academically
- **What to know:** Your academic advisor is your ally. They know the requirements, the policies, and often have insights about professors and courses. Build a relationship with them early.

**Course-Specific Tutoring**
- **What it is:** One-on-one or small group tutoring for specific courses (usually math, science, writing, languages)
- **Who uses it:** Students who want extra help or are struggling in a class
- **When to go:** As soon as you realize you could use help (not the night before the exam)
- **What to know:** Tutoring works best when you're engaged—asking questions, practicing, not just passively listening.

**Writing Center / Writing Support**
- **What it is:** Help with writing—brainstorming, organizing, drafting, editing papers (for any class, on any topic)
- **Who uses it:** Writers at all levels. Seriously. Even "good" writers benefit.
- **When to go:** Early in the writing process (not just proofreading at the end)
- **What to know:** These aren't just for fixing grammar. They help you think through your ideas, strengthen your arguments, and develop as a writer.

**Subject-Specific Learning Centers**
- **What it is:** Support specific to particular subjects (Math Lab, Science Center, etc.)
- **Who uses it:** Students in those subjects who want extra practice, explanation, or support
- **When to go:** When you're confused or want to deepen understanding
- **What to know:** Many offer drop-in help, so you can go when you need it, not just by appointment.

**Disability Services / Accessibility Office**
- **What it is:** Support for students with documented disabilities (physical, learning, mental health, etc.)
- **Who uses it:** Students with disabilities who need accommodations
- **When to go:** Early in the semester (so accommodations can be set in place for your classes)
- **What to know:** Having a disability doesn't make you "less than." Accommodations level the playing field. This office is there to help you succeed.

---

### Personal & Mental Health Support

College is emotional. You'll experience stress, anxiety, loneliness, depression, identity questions. That's not weakness—it's part of being human, in a big transition, with new demands.

**Counseling Services / Mental Health**
- **What it is:** Professional counseling with licensed therapists or counselors
- **Who uses it:** Anyone dealing with stress, anxiety, depression, relationship issues, trauma, identity questions, or just wanting to talk to someone
- **When to go:** Anytime. Seriously. You don't have to be in crisis. Many students go regularly for ongoing support.
- **What to know:** It's confidential. What you say stays between you and your counselor (with very limited exceptions). Many colleges offer free or low-cost sessions. Use them.

**Crisis Support**
- **What it is:** 24/7 emergency support if you're in crisis (suicidal thoughts, severe trauma, dangerous situations)
- **Who uses it:** Anyone in acute crisis
- **When to go:** Immediately, if you ever need it
- **What to know:** There's always someone available. This is judgment-free. You won't get in trouble for reaching out.

**Wellness Programs**
- **What it is:** Programs focused on physical health, stress management, sleep, nutrition, fitness
- **Who uses it:** Anyone wanting to build healthier habits
- **When to go:** Whenever you want to learn about or improve wellness
- **What to know:** Many are free group classes, workshops, or drop-in support. Low pressure, high impact.

---

### Identity & Community Support

Most colleges have offices and communities focused on student identity and belonging.

**Cultural Centers / Identity Offices**
- **What it is:** Communities and resources for students of specific identities (BIPOC students, LGBTQ+ students, first-generation students, international students, etc.)
- **Who uses it:** Students exploring their identity or seeking community with people who share similar experiences
- **When to go:** Anytime, to attend events, find community, or get cultural resources
- **What to know:** These spaces are designed to support you and help you feel less alone. They're often hubs of real community.

**Spiritual / Interfaith Office**
- **What it is:** Support for students' spiritual, religious, or faith-based needs
- **Who uses it:** Students of any faith tradition (or no faith) wanting spiritual community or guidance
- **When to go:** If faith is important to you, or you're exploring spirituality
- **What to know:** Most colleges welcome all traditions and beliefs. This office can connect you to communities and resources.

**Student Life / Residential Life**
- **What it is:** Programming, community-building, student organizations, leadership opportunities
- **Who uses it:** Anyone wanting to get involved, find community, or develop leadership skills
- **When to go:** Anytime to explore what's available
- **What to know:** Involvement on campus is correlated with happiness, retention, and success. It matters.

---

### Career & Academic Planning Support

We'll talk more about career development in Module 12, but these resources exist for planning your path.

**Career Services**
- **What it is:** Help with career exploration, resume building, interview prep, job searching, internship finding
- **Who uses it:** Students thinking about careers (yes, even as a first-year student)
- **When to go:** Start of college for exploration; junior year for serious job prep; anytime you're considering a path
- **What to know:** Career advisors are great at helping you connect your skills and interests to possibilities. They have job boards, employer connections, and insider knowledge.

**Internship / Work-Study Offices**
- **What it is:** Connections to paid work, internships, and volunteer opportunities
- **Who uses it:** Students wanting practical experience and income
- **When to go:** When you want to add work experience to your college career
- **What to know:** Work-study and internships aren't just for making money. They're resume-builders and exploration opportunities.

---

### Specialized Support

Depending on your situation, other resources might matter:

**Financial Aid Office**
- **What it is:** Help with loans, grants, scholarships, financial planning, and FAFSA
- **Who uses it:** Anyone paying for college (basically everyone)
- **When to go:** At the start of each year for aid; anytime you have questions about finances
- **What to know:** These folks know the system and can help you access funding and understand your options.

**International Student Services**
- **What it is:** Support for international students navigating visas, academics, cultural adjustment
- **Who uses it:** International students
- **When to go:** Early on for visa/legal stuff; anytime for support with the college transition

**First-Generation Support Programs**
- **What it is:** Programs specifically for students whose parents didn't attend college
- **Who uses it:** First-gen students
- **When to go:** Great for mentorship, community, and navigating the college system

**LGBTQ+ Center / Resource**
- **What it is:** Community, advocacy, and resources for LGBTQ+ students
- **Who uses it:** LGBTQ+ students and allies
- **When to go:** Anytime to find community, attend events, or access resources

## The Real Talk: Why Students Don't Seek Help

I want to name some of the barriers you might face. Because knowing them exist? That helps.

**"I don't want to bother anyone."**
This is the biggest one. You think someone is busy, or that your problem isn't "big enough." Here's the truth: These people exist to help. You reaching out isn't a burden—it's what they signed up for. Let them do their job.

**"I should be able to figure this out myself."**
Nope. Seeking help isn't a personal failure. It's a smart strategy. Every successful person has a support network. You're not unique in needing help.

**"It will be awkward / I'll be judged."**
Vulnerability takes courage. And yeah, it might feel awkward the first time. But these professionals hear similar stories constantly. You won't shock them. And they're trained to be non-judgmental.

**"I don't know where to go."**
Valid. That's why we're doing this module. By the end, you'll have a map.

**"I've tried asking for help before and it didn't help."**
Sometimes the first person or place isn't the right fit. That's not failure. That's data. Try someone else. Keep looking.

**"I'm worried about what will go in my record."**
Most support is confidential. Academic stuff goes in your file, but that's to help you (documentation of accommodations, etc.). Mental health? That's private. Reaching out to a counselor won't affect your academics or future.

**"I'm too proud."**
I get it. But pride often comes before a fall. The strongest thing you can do is ask for help.

## How to Actually Ask for Help (It's a Skill)

Okay, so you've decided to reach out. Now what? Here's how to do it effectively.

### Step 1: Identify What You Need

Before you go talk to someone, get clear on:
- **What's the issue?** (I'm struggling in my math class / My anxiety is getting worse / I'm not sure about my major)
- **Why is it a problem?** (It's affecting my grades / It's affecting my ability to function / It's creating decision paralysis)
- **What kind of help would actually help?** (Tutoring / Counseling / Academic advising / Skill coaching)

You don't need to be perfectly articulate. But having even a rough sense helps the person you're talking to point you in the right direction.

### Step 2: Find the Right Resource

Use the list above. Look at your college's website. Search "[Your College] academic support" or "[Your College] counseling services."

**Pro tip:** If you're not 100% sure where to go, start with your academic advisor or student life office. They can point you to the right place.

### Step 3: Make the First Contact

**In person** (if you like direct engagement):
- Walk into the office during drop-in hours
- Say something like: "Hi, I'm [your name], a [year] at [college]. I'm struggling with [X] and I'm looking for help. Can someone talk to me about what you offer?"

**By email** (if you like having a written record):
- Subject: "Initial Consultation Request - [Brief description]"
- Body: Keep it simple and clear. See the template below.

**By phone** (if your office offers it):
- Call during office hours
- Be ready to briefly explain what you need

### Step 4: What to Expect in That First Meeting

Usually, it goes something like:
1. **Introduction** — They ask your name, major, year
2. **The Issue** — You explain what's going on (don't overthink it; explain it like you're talking to a friend)
3. **Questions** — They ask clarifying questions
4. **Options** — They explain what they can offer, and what else might help
5. **Next Steps** — You decide together what makes sense
6. **Logistics** — You schedule a follow-up or get resources

**Expect to feel a little nervous.** That's normal. Most people are kind and patient.

### Sample Email Template

Here's a template you can adapt:

---

**Subject Line:** Initial Consultation Request - [Math Tutoring / Counseling / Academic Planning / etc.]

Hi [Name/Office],

My name is [Your Name] and I'm a [first-year/sophomore/etc.] [major] at [College]. I'm reaching out because I'm struggling with [briefly describe the issue: math class / anxiety and stress / choosing my major / etc.], and I'd like to connect with someone who can help.

I'm available [list general availability: mornings, afternoons, evenings, etc.], and I'm happy to work around your schedule.

Thank you for your time, and I look forward to connecting.

Best,
[Your Name]
[Phone Number if you're comfortable]

---

See? Not complicated. Just honest and clear.

## Building Your Personal Support Network

Here's the thing: **Don't wait until you're in crisis to build relationships with support people.**

Think of it like this—if you only reach out to a friend when you need something, your relationship feels transactional. But if you've built a real connection, they're happy to help.

Same with campus resources. If you meet with your academic advisor early (even just to say hi and talk about your goals), then when you're struggling, that relationship is already warm.

### Your Support Network Checklist

By the end of this module, you should know:

**Learning & Academic Support:**
- [ ] Your academic advisor's name and contact info
- [ ] Where to find writing support
- [ ] Where to find tutoring (especially in classes you're taking this semester)
- [ ] How disability services works (even if you don't think you need it now)

**Personal & Mental Health:**
- [ ] Where the counseling center is
- [ ] How many free sessions you get per semester
- [ ] How to access crisis support 24/7
- [ ] One wellness program or class you might try

**Community & Belonging:**
- [ ] At least one student organization or cultural center that interests you
- [ ] How to find information about campus events
- [ ] One community of students you might want to join

**Career & Planning:**
- [ ] Where career services is
- [ ] What services they offer
- [ ] At least one person (advisor, mentor, etc.) who can help you think through your path

This isn't about immediately using all of these. It's about knowing they're there.

## Discussion Prompts & Reflection

Okay, let's get real about your help-seeking.

### Individual Reflection (Do This First)

Spend 15-20 minutes on these:

**1. Your Help-Seeking History**
- When have you asked for help in the past? (Any domain—school, friendship, family, sports, etc.)
- What happened? Did it help?
- How did it feel to ask?

**2. Your Barriers**
From the list above, which barriers resonate with you?
- "I don't want to bother anyone"
- "I should figure this out myself"
- "It will be awkward"
- "I don't know where to go"
- "I've tried before and it didn't help"
- "I'm worried about my record"
- "I'm too proud"
- Or something else?

What makes help-seeking hard *for you specifically*?

**3. Reframing**
If you shift your perspective, how could asking for help be a sign of strength rather than weakness?

What would it look like to "be strong" *and* ask for help?

**4. Your Support Network Right Now**
Who do you have right now that you can lean on? (Friends, family, etc.)

Who else might you be able to add?

### Online Discussion: Break the Silence

The more we talk openly about needing help, the more normal it becomes.

**Prompt 1: Your Help-Seeking Story**
Share about a time you asked for help (in any area of life). What happened? What did you learn?

**Prompt 2: Your Barrier**
What makes help-seeking hard for you? Be honest. You're likely not alone in this.

**Prompt 3: Who's in Your Network?**
Besides your family, name one person you can count on right now. What makes them "a person you can count on"?

Respond to at least two peers. Share what resonates. Offer encouragement.

## Practical Work This Week

This is the week you actually get connected.

### Activity 1: Campus Resource Mapping (1.5 hours)

On your college's website, find and document:

1. **Academic Support**
   - Your advisor / academic advising office (name, email, office location)
   - Writing center (location, hours, how to book an appointment)
   - Tutoring (for at least 2 subjects you're taking)
   - Disability services

2. **Mental Health & Wellness**
   - Counseling center (phone number, how to schedule)
   - Crisis line (24/7 number)
   - Wellness center or health services

3. **Community & Engagement**
   - Student life office
   - At least 2 student organizations or communities you're curious about

4. **Career & Planning**
   - Career services (location, what they offer)

**Pro tip:** Bookmark or save these. Put them in your phone. You might print them out and post them above your desk.

**Create a simple document or note** with this info that you can access anytime.

### Activity 2: Make One Connection (1 hour)

Pick ONE of the resources above. Make the first contact this week.

This could be:
- [ ] Email your academic advisor introducing yourself
- [ ] Schedule a writers' center appointment (even if you don't have a paper due yet)
- [ ] Email the counseling center asking how to schedule an initial consultation
- [ ] Visit a student organization's meeting or event
- [ ] Call or email career services with a question

**The goal:** Get past the "reaching out" step. See that it's not scary.

**What to expect after:** You might not hear back immediately. That's normal. Follow up if you don't hear back in a week.

### Activity 3: Draft an Email Seeking Help (30 min)

Using the template I provided above, write an email seeking help for something you're actually struggling with (or think you might struggle with).

You don't have to send it yet. But practicing this skill in a low-stakes way helps.

Once you've written it, read it. Does it feel clear? Would it prompt the response you want?

After this module, if you need help with something, you already have this drafted. You can adapt and send it.

### Activity 4: Reflection & Discussion (1 hour)

- [ ] Do the individual reflection prompts above (20 min)
- [ ] Post to the discussion forum (15 min)
- [ ] Read and respond to at least 2 peers (15 min)

## A Few Things to Remember

**1. Everyone uses these resources.**
Walking into the counseling center or writing center doesn't make you "a problem student." It makes you a *strategic* student.

**2. It gets easier.**
The first time you ask for help is the hardest. After that? You'll wonder why you were nervous.

**3. There's always someone available.**
Even at 2 AM on a Saturday. Even on a holiday. Even if you think your problem is "stupid." There's someone there.

**4. This is what these resources exist for.**
Academic advisors, tutors, counselors, career coaches—they chose this work because they want to help students. Use them. They want to help you.

**5. Asking for help is a learned skill.**
You're not born knowing how to do it. You're learning. That's okay.

## Key Takeaways This Week

- **Everyone needs help sometimes.** Using college resources is strategic, not weak.
- **Your college has extensive support systems** across academics, mental health, career, and community.
- **Help-seeking is a skill you can develop.** Start small. Build relationships. Get more comfortable.
- **Don't wait until crisis mode.** Connect now, while things are manageable, so you're not alone when they're hard.
- **Knowing where support lives is power.** You've got this information now. Use it.
