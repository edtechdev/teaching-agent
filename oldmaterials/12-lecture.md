# Module 12: Career Development & Exploration

## From Purpose to Path: Exploring Your Career

You have your purpose statement. You have your education plan mapped out.

Now: **What's the career direction you're building toward?**

This week is about exploration. Not having it all figured out. Exploration.

---

## Why Career Exploration Matters (Right Now)

You might be thinking: "I have time. I don't need to think about careers yet."

Here's the thing: **Your courses, your internships, your experiences—they're all building toward something.** Being intentional about the direction helps you make better choices *now*.

It doesn't mean you're locked in. But it means:
- You choose internships strategically
- You pick electives that build relevant skills
- You pursue projects and opportunities that matter
- You make the most of your time in college

So yes, explore. Now. While you're here.

---

## What a Career Actually Is

Before we go forward, let's clarify something:

**A career isn't a job you get at 22 and keep forever.**

A career is:
- The evolving path of work and experiences you build
- The skills and expertise you develop
- The impact you have through work
- The values you bring to what you do

For most people, this looks like:
- Different jobs over time (maybe 6-10+ different roles in a lifetime)
- Skill development that makes you more valuable
- Your interests and priorities shifting
- Your impact potentially growing or changing

**So when we're exploring careers now, we're not deciding "I'll do THIS EXACT JOB FOREVER."**

We're asking: "What kinds of work align with my values? What problems interest me? What skills do I want to develop? What kind of impact do I want to have?"

## Career Development: Self-Assessment First

Before exploring specific careers, understand yourself better.

### What Energizes You?

Think about times when you've felt most engaged, alive, in the zone.
- What were you doing?
- Who were you with?
- What were you solving or creating?

**This matters because:** If you spend 40+ hours per week doing work that doesn't energize you, you'll feel it.

### What Are Your Values?

From your purpose statement, you already have some clarity on this. But let's go deeper.

What matters to you in work?

Common work values include:
- **Impact:** Helping others, solving problems, making change
- **Mastery:** Getting really good at something, continuous learning
- **Autonomy:** Having control over how you work
- **Community:** Working with people you respect
- **Stability:** Predictable income, secure future
- **Creativity:** Making new things, expressing yourself
- **Flexibility:** Time with family, pursuing other interests
- **Recognition:** Being valued and appreciated
- **Financial security:** Earning enough to support yourself and maybe others
- **Meaning:** Aligned with your worldview, your cause

What 3-5 of these matter most to you?

### What Problems Interest You?

When do you read an article and think "this matters" or "I want to understand this better"?

Maybe you care about:
- Education access
- Environmental sustainability
- Mental health
- Economic justice
- Technology and its impact
- Healthcare
- Criminal justice reform
- Business innovation
- Artistic expression
- Scientific discovery

These aren't all huge societal problems. Maybe you're interested in how companies work. Maybe you love helping individual clients solve problems. Maybe you're fascinated by design. Maybe you care about how families function.

**Where is your curiosity?** Follow that.

### What Are You Good At?

Not just academically. But in general.

Are you good at:
- Working with people?
- Analyzing data?
- Creating things?
- Organizing systems?
- Presenting ideas?
- Building things?
- Listening to people?
- Solving problems under pressure?
- Teaching?
- Writing?

This matters because: **You're likely to enjoy and succeed in careers where you use your natural strengths.**

Not because you're limited to only these strengths—skills develop—but because starting with what you're already good at gives you confidence and momentum.

## Career Exploration: The Landscape

Once you've done some self-assessment, it's time to explore.

### Method 1: Industry/Sector Exploration

Pick an industry that interests you:
- Tech
- Education
- Healthcare
- Finance
- Nonprofit/NGO sector
- Government
- Arts and entertainment
- Business
- Manufacturing
- Etc.

Then ask:

**"Within this industry, what roles exist?"**

No job is just one role. There are dozens of paths. In healthcare, for example:
- Direct patient care (doctor, nurse, therapist)
- Healthcare administration
- Medical research
- Healthcare policy
- Healthcare technology
- Hospital management
- Etc.

Each has very different work.

### Method 2: Function-Based Exploration

Instead of industry, pick a *function* (what you'd do day-to-day):

- Sales/business development
- Marketing
- Data analysis
- Software development
- Project management
- Teaching/training
- Design
- Writing/communications
- Human resources
- Operations
- Research
- Consulting
- Etc.

Then ask: **"In what industries does this function exist?"**

Someone doing data analysis might work in:
- Tech
- Finance
- Healthcare
- Government
- Nonprofits
- Sports
- Education
- Etc.

### Method 3: Values-First Exploration

Pick a value that matters to you:
- "I want to help others directly" (social services, healthcare, teaching, ministry)
- "I want to solve environmental problems" (environmental science, green tech, policy, sustainability consulting)
- "I want to create things" (art, design, writing, engineering, product development)
- "I want to understand how things work" (research, analysis, engineering)
- "I want to build a business" (entrepreneurship, business development)

Then ask: **"What careers align with this value?"**

## Exploring Specific Careers: Go Deep

Once you've narrowed your range, pick 2-3 specific careers to explore more deeply.

For each career, research:

### What Does the Work Actually Involve?

- Day-to-day tasks (not the fancy job title, but what does someone actually *do*)
- Work environment (office? field? travel? remote?)
- Schedule (typical hours, flexibility)
- Collaboration (solo work or team-based?)

### What Skills and Education Are Needed?

- Typical degree level (bachelor's, master's, certificate?)
- Specific knowledge or training
- Soft skills that matter (communication, leadership, analytical thinking, etc.)
- How you build credibility in the field (certifications, experience, etc.)

### What's the Career Path?

- How do people typically enter this field?
- What's early career like? (entry-level roles)
- How do people advance?
- What does mid-career and late-career look like?
- How long before you feel competent?

### What Are the Realities?

- Job market (is it growing, stable, shrinking?)
- Compensation (realistic salary ranges)
- Job satisfaction (what do people actually enjoy? What's frustrating?)
- Work-life balance (realistic expectations)
- Geographic requirements (do you have to be in certain locations?)

### Talk to Someone Doing It

This is crucial. **Talk to an actual person in the career.**

Not a company recruiter trying to sell you. An actual person doing the work.

How?
- LinkedIn (message someone and ask for an informational interview)
- Informational interviews through your college's alumni network
- Professional associations in the field
- Internship supervisors
- Professors in relevant departments
- Icebreaker at career fairs (even 10-minute conversations help)

What to ask:
- "What does a typical day look like?"
- "What do you love about this work?"
- "What surprised you about this career?"
- "What would you tell someone considering this field?"
- "What skills matter most?"
- "How did you get into this work?"

**Listen more than you talk.** This is about learning, not impressing.

## Building Career-Focused Experience While in College

Once you have some direction, **use your remaining college time strategically.**

### Internships

The best way to explore a career without committing full-time is an internship.

- Try internships in the fields you're considering
- Each internship teaches you something (sometimes "this isn't for me" is valuable learning)
- Later internships can be more targeted and substantive
- Internships build resume credentials and professional networks

**Timeline:** Usually sophomore or junior year. You want some foundation of knowledge first, but you want time to explore multiple options.

### Projects and Research

- Look for relevant class projects, undergraduate research, or directed studies
- Community-based projects aligned with your interests
- These build skills and knowledge in your field

### Professional Involvement

- Clubs and organizations related to your field (psychology club, engineering society, entrepreneurship organization, etc.)
- Leadership roles in these organizations
- Professional conferences or events in your field

### Service and Volunteering

- Volunteer in roles related to your interests
- This is exploration AND service AND resume-building

### Building Your Network

- Connect with professionals in your field (LinkedIn)
- Attend career fairs and industry events
- Participate in alumni events
- Maintain relationships with professors and internship supervisors

These connections matter way more than most students realize. **Jobs are often filled through networks, not job postings.**

## The Careers You're Considering Now Might Change

And that's okay.

Some of your classmates will change their major. Some will get a totally different job than they planned. Some will have careers that don't match their degree at all.

This is normal. **The goal isn't to predict your entire career. The goal is to stay intentional and keep learning.**

Your exploration this year might lead somewhere different next year. That's not failure. That's you gathering information and adjusting your direction.

## Your Action Plan This Week (Week 12)

### Activity 1: Self-Assessment (45 min)

- Complete the individual reflection prompts above
- Write down what energizes you, your values, and your curiosity
- Be honest—this is for you, not for anyone else

### Activity 2: Research 2-3 Careers (2 hours)

- Pick 2-3 specific careers to explore
- For each one:
  - Research the day-to-day work
  - Find entry-level roles
  - Look at typical career progression
  - Research compensation and job market

- **Where to research:**
  - O*NET Online (comprehensive career database)
  - Bureau of Labor Statistics (job market data)
  - LinkedIn (see what actual jobs are posted)
  - Glassdoor (salary and company insights)
  - Professional association websites
  - Informational blogs and YouTube channels from people in the field

### Activity 3: Set Up Informational Interviews (30 min)

- Pick one person in one of your target careers
- Find them through:
  - Your college's alumni network
  - LinkedIn
  - Professional associations
  - Asking professors or advisors
  - Your internship supervisor if they're in a field you're considering

- Message them with a short, respectful request:
  - "Hi [name], I'm a [year] student at [college] considering a career in [field]. Would you have 20-30 minutes for a brief informational interview? I'd love to learn from your experience."

- Schedule for the next few weeks (not necessarily this week)

### Activity 4: Explore Campus Resources (30 min)

- Visit your career services center
- Ask about:
  - Career testing (Myers-Briggs, Strong Interest Inventory, etc.)
  - Internship opportunities in your target field
  - Alumni mentor connections
  - Interview preparation
  - Resume help

### Activity 5: Engage in Discussion (1 hour)

- Answer the reflection prompts
- Post to the forum
- Respond to at least 2 peers

### Activity 6: Plan Your Next Steps (15 min)

- Based on this week's exploration, what's your next move?
- When will you do an informational interview?
- When might you pursue an internship in this field?
- What courses or skills do you need to develop?

## Key Takeaways This Week

- **Career exploration is a skill.** You're learning to research, question, and evaluate.
- **Your interests are valid.** Go where your curiosity leads.
- **The best information comes from people.** Spend time learning from folks actually doing the work.
- **Career paths aren't linear.** They twist and turn, and that's okay.
- **You're building this intentionally.** Through your courses, internships, and experiences, you're developing relevant skills and knowledge.
