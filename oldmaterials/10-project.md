# Module 10: Understanding Your Academic Path

## Your Education Is a Choice, Not a Prescription

Here's something important to understand: **Your education isn't something that happens to you. It's something you design.**

Yes, you have degree requirements. Yes, there are prerequisites and structures. Yes, there are probably some "you have to take these" classes.

But within that framework, you have *choices*. Thousands of them.

Which courses match your interests? Which professors inspire you? Which sequences make sense for your timeline? Which experiences (internships, research, study abroad) do you want to include?

This module is about getting clear on the landscape so you can make intentional choices.

---

## Understanding Your Degree Requirements

First, let's demystify your actual requirements.

### The Big Picture

Most bachelor's degrees require roughly 120 credit hours (this varies) across several categories:

**General Education / Core Requirements**
- Courses that all students take (writing, math, science, humanities, etc.)
- Usually about 30-45 credits
- Purpose: Broad learning and foundational skills

**Major Requirements**
- Courses specific to your field of study
- Usually about 40-60 credits
- Purpose: Deep learning in your chosen discipline

**Electives / Free Courses**
- You choose these based on interests
- Usually about 20-40 credits
- Purpose: Explore adjacent fields and deepen learning in areas you care about

**Some special opportunities:**
- Minor (like a major, but less requirements—usually 15-20 credits)
- Certificate program (shorter, focused credential)
- Double major (two full majors—requires more credits)
- Independent study, research, or honors options

### Reading Your Degree Audit

Your college has a document (sometimes called a "degree audit" or "evaluation") that shows:
- What you've already completed
- What you still need
- How courses count toward different requirements

This is your roadmap.

**How to find and use it:**
- Ask your academic advisor
- Check your college's student portal or degree audit system
- Literally print it out and annotate it
- This is not a boring form—this is your path forward

**Pro move:** Understand which requirements are *flexible* (you can choose from a list) and which are *fixed* (specific course required).

### Beyond Requirements: Choosing Electives

Here's where your real education happens: **in the courses you choose because you're curious.**

You're not just checking boxes. You're building knowledge in areas that matter to you.

**When selecting electives:**
- What genuinely interests you?
- What skills do you want to develop?
- How does this connect to your career interests?
- What would you regret NOT learning while you're here?

Don't fill these slots with easy classes just to graduate faster. You'll regret it.

## Exploring Majors & Minors

Maybe you came in with a major already decided. Maybe you're still figuring it out. Either way, this matters.

### Understanding Your Major

Your major isn't necessarily what you'll do for the rest of your life. It's not a life sentence. It's what you're studying *right now* and how you're spending your college years.

**Different majors offer different things:**

**STEM (Science, Technology, Engineering, Math)**
- Deep technical knowledge
- Strong job market and earning potential
- Can be challenging and time-intensive
- Opens doors to many career paths
- Usually has more rigid requirements

**Humanities (Literature, Philosophy, History, Languages)**
- Deep critical thinking skills
- Cultural and historical understanding
- Excellent writing and communication skills
- More flexible and fewer rigid requirements
- Less directly connected to specific jobs (but skills are highly valuable)

**Social Sciences (Psychology, Sociology, Economics, Political Science)**
- Understanding human behavior and society
- Research and analytical skills
- Broader range of career applications
- Mix of flexible and required courses

**Business & Professional Programs**
- Direct job preparation
- Networking and internship opportunities
- Clear career pathways
- Professional focus but still broad learning

**Creative Fields (Art, Music, Theater, Film)**
- Portfolio building
- Creative skill development
- Community and collaboration
- Often require significant time and practice
- Competitive but rewarding

**The reality:** Every major can lead to lots of different careers. And every major has value that extends beyond a single career path.

### The Minor Option

A minor is like a "focused exploration" of a field. Usually 15-20 credits (5-7 classes) in a related field.

**Why minors matter:**
- They show depth in an area beyond your major (valuable on resumes)
- They let you explore without committing to a full second major
- They can complement your major in interesting ways (e.g., Math major + Music minor)
- They might open career doors (e.g., Biology major + Chemistry minor for certain grad programs)
- They let you follow your curiosity

**When they're less useful:**
- If they're a distraction from your major
- If they're just "because it sounds good" without real interest
- If they prevent you from exploring through electives
- If they stress you out

## Sequencing Your Courses

Okay, so you know what you have to take and what options you have. Now, how do you arrange them over semesters?

### The Strategic Approach

**Front-load your challenges:**
- Take hard classes when you're fresh (not senior year when senioritis hits)
- Spread challenging classes so you're not overloaded
- If you're pre-med or pre-law, front-load those harder courses

**Create semester rhythm:**
- Mix hard classes with more manageable ones
- Include at least one class you're genuinely interested in each semester
- Balance writing-heavy courses (if that's a challenge for you) with other types

**Consider timing:**
- When are popular classes offered?
- Are there prerequisites that force certain sequences?
- When can you involve internships or research?
- When do you want to study abroad (if that's a goal)?

**Think ahead:**
- Senior year: Are you doing a capstone, thesis, or final project?
- Summer: Internship, research, or time off?
- Grad school timeline: If you're considering grad school, what do you need on your transcript?

### Example Semester Plans

**Semester 1 (First-year):**
- One hard core class (e.g., Calculus, Chemistry)
- One writing/humanities class (develops core skills)
- One introductory major class (see if you like the field)
- One elective of genuine interest
- One lighter core requirement

**Semester 3-4 (Sophomore):**
- Deepen major courses
- Complete more core requirements
- One elective exploring a possible minor
- Maintain balance

**Semester 5-6 (Junior):**
- Focus on major depth (including higher-level courses)
- Possibly add a research, internship, or study abroad experience
- Cap off general education
- Explore through electives

**Semester 7-8 (Senior):**
- Finish major requirements
- Capstone or thesis (if required)
- Electives that connect to career interests
- Lighter load to make space for career prep (resume, interviews, etc.)

## Academic Opportunities Beyond Courses

College offers so much beyond sitting in classes.

### Research & Internships

**Undergraduate Research**
- Many professors do research and bring students on
- Usually happens through: asking a professor, applying to a program, or getting connected through your department
- Benefits: Deep learning, building relationships with professors, resume builder, possible publication
- When: Often sophomore year or later, though some programs start freshman year

**Internships**
- Paid or unpaid work experience in a field
- Usually summer, some during semester
- Benefits: Practical experience, networking, resume builder, sometimes course credit
- When: Usually sophomore year or later, though some companies have freshman internships
- Connection: Your college's career center usually has listings

**Study Abroad**
- Taking courses or interning in another country
- Usually a semester or summer
- Benefits: Cultural immersion, global perspective, personal growth, adventure
- When: Usually sophomore-junior year so you have prerequisites done
- Connection: Your college's study abroad office

### Leadership & Involvement

- Student organizations and clubs
- Leadership positions in organizations
- Resident advisor or community roles
- Important: These can be resume builders, but they're also valuable for belonging and growth

## Conversations to Have

Before you lock in your plan, have some conversations.

### With Your Academic Advisor

"Here's my major and what I'm interested in. How would you sequence my courses over the next [X] years given my goals?"

They'll know:
- What courses are usually available
- What the prerequisites are
- Which professors are exceptional
- How to best set you up

### With Faculty in Your Major

Stand out in class. Then:
- "What advice do you have for students in this major?"
- "What courses in our major do you think are essential?"
- "Are there research opportunities you'd recommend?"

They can give insider knowledge.

### With Your Career Services Office

Even if you're not sure about your career yet:
- "I'm a [major] and interested in [rough area]. What courses or experiences would strengthen my candidacy?"
- "What internships do employers in this field typically look for?"

They connect education to careers.

## Discussion Prompts & Reflection

Let's get intentional about your academic path.

### Individual Reflection (Do This First)

**1. Your Degree Requirements**
- Do you have a copy of your degree audit or requirements sheet?
- If not: How will you get one?
- If yes: What are you surprised by? What's clear? What's confusing?

**2. Your Major**
- What's your major (or are you still exploring)?
- What drew you to it?
- Does it still feel right?

**3. Your Sequencing**
- How many semesters do you have left?
- What hard requirements do you still need?
- What electives are you curious about?
- What would a rough plan for your remaining semesters look like?

**4. Opportunities**
- Are you interested in internships? Research? Study abroad?
- When might you do those?
- What would you need to make them happen?

**5. Conversations**
- Have you talked to your academic advisor?
- Have you talked to faculty in your major?
- What questions do you have?

### Online Discussion: Chart Your Path

**Prompt 1: Your Major Story**
Why did you choose your major? What drew you to it? (Or, if you're still deciding, what majors are you considering and why?)

**Prompt 2: The Course You're Excited About**
What's a course you're planning to take (or have taken) that you're genuinely excited about? Why?

**Prompt 3: Your Opportunity**
Are you interested in internships, research, study abroad, or other opportunities? What appeals to you?

Respond to at least two peers. Share what resonates. Offer suggestions.

## Your Action Plan This Week

### Activity 1: Gather Your Roadmap (30 min)

- Get your degree audit or requirements sheet
- Print it out or save it on your computer
- Read it carefully
- Highlight what you've already done, what you need to do, and what's flexible

### Activity 2: Map Your Remaining Semesters (1 hour)

- For each semester you have left, list:
  - Fixed requirements you need to take
  - Courses you're considering
  - Any special opportunities (internship, research, etc.)
- Create a rough draft plan
- Don't overthink it—you'll refine this next week

### Activity 3: Academic Advisor Conversation (varies)

- Schedule a meeting with your academic advisor
- Bring your rough plan and questions
- Conversation prompts: "How would you sequence my courses given these goals?" "What opportunities should I consider?" "Which courses in my major are most important?"
- Take notes

### Activity 4: Major Exploration (if undecided) (1-2 hours)

If you're still exploring majors:
- Take a major/career assessment (your college's career center probably has these)
- Talk to a few professors in different departments
- "Shadow" courses in different majors (sit in on lectures)
- See what resonates

### Activity 5: Engage in Discussion (1 hour)

- Do reflection prompts
- Post to forum
- Respond to peers

## Key Takeaways This Week

- **Your degree has requirements, but within those, you have choices.** Understand the landscape so you can choose intentionally.
- **Your major matters for the next 2-4 years.** It doesn't determine your entire future, but it matters for now.
- **Sequencing matters.** Front-load challenges, balance your semesters, think ahead about internships and special opportunities.
- **Beyond courses, there's so much more.** Research, internships, study abroad—these are part of your education.
- **You're not alone in this planning.** Advisors, faculty, career services—they want to help.
