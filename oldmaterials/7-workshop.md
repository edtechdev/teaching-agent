# Module 7: Time Management & Productivity

## You Have the Same 24 Hours as Everyone Else

Let me start with something real: **Time management is not about managing time. It's about managing yourself.**

You can't actually manage time. An hour is an hour. What you can manage is:
- What you choose to do in each hour
- Where you focus your attention
- What you prioritize
- Your energy and focus

Every successful student you know isn't successful because they have more time. They're successful because they use their time intentionally.

If you're struggling to get everything done, it's probably not that you "suck at time management." It's that you're either:
- Trying to do too much
- Procrastinating (which we'll talk about)
- Taking on things that aren't aligned with your goals
- Not realizing where your time is actually going

This week, we fix that.

## The Time Audit: Know Where Your Time Actually Goes

Before you can manage your time, you have to know where it's going.

Most students have no idea. They think they study 20 hours a week when they really study 3. Or they think they have no free time when they have pockets they're not seeing.

### How to Do a Time Audit

For **3-5 days**, track everything you do and how long it takes.

Yes, this is tedious. Do it anyway. It takes about 10 minutes per day and it'll change everything.

**How:**
- Every hour (or every few hours), write down what you did and how long it took
- Be honest, not aspirational ("I woke up and it took 20 minutes, not 10")
- Include: classes, studying, eating, working, exercising, socializing, scrolling, sleeping, everything
- At the end, add it all up by category

**You'll discover things like:**
- "I spend 2 hours a day on social media" (or more)
- "I only actually study 8 hours a week when I thought it was 15"
- "I'm spending X hours commuting"
- "I have X hours completely free but I didn't realize it"

This data is powerful. And it's honest.

## Priorities: What Actually Matters?

Once you know where your time goes, you need to know where it *should* go.

This has to align with your purpose statement (remember Module 2?). And it has to be realistic.

### The Big Buckets

Most students need to allocate time across several domains:

**Non-negotiable:**
- Classes (going to them)
- Sleep (7-9 hours)
- Basic self-care (showering, eating, etc.)
- Part-time job (if you have one)
- Essential responsibilities (family obligations, health issues, etc.)

**Important (your college success):**
- Studying and coursework outside of class
- Project work and assignments
- Group work and study groups

**Important (your life and sanity):**
- Exercise and movement
- Social connection and community
- Mental health practices (meditation, therapy, etc.)
- Hobbies and things that bring you joy

**Optional (but often take up time):**
- Extra commitments (multiple clubs, leadership positions, etc.)
- Side projects
- Content consumption (TV, movies, YouTube)
- Social media and scrolling

**The reality:** You can't do everything. You have to choose.

### The Priorities Conversation

Here's what I want you to do:

**Think about this semester.** What are your top 3-5 priorities?

Maybe:
- Do well in my major classes
- Maintain my mental health
- Build community and belonging
- Work 20 hours a week to pay for school
- Make progress on my purpose statement goals

**Not:** Everything. Just the things that matter most right now.

Once you have your priorities, your time allocation makes sense. You allocate time to the things that matter.

## Time Management Frameworks

There are several approaches to managing time. Here are the most useful ones for students.

### 1. Time Blocking

**What it is:** You schedule specific blocks of time for specific activities.

**How it works:**
```
8:00-9:00 AM: Sleep
9:00-10:00 AM: Breakfast & get ready
10:00-11:30 AM: Math class
11:30 AM-12:00 PM: Lunch
12:00-2:00 PM: Study for chemistry exam
2:00-3:00 PM: Work
3:00-4:30 PM: Biology lab
4:30-6:00 PM: Dinner & break
6:00-7:30 PM: Homework for English
7:30-9:00 PM: Group study
9:00-10:30 PM: Personal time
10:30-11:30 PM: Wind down & sleep
```

**Pros:**
- Very clear and structured
- Visual representation of your day
- Helps prevent overcommitment
- Easy to see where time goes
- Works well for people who like structure

**Cons:**
- Rigid (real life doesn't always fit blocks)
- Can feel restrictive
- Hard to adjust mid-week if plans change
- Requires some initial setup

**Best for:** People who like structure, busy students, visualizers

### 2. The Eisenhower Matrix (Urgent vs. Important)

**What it is:** You categorize tasks by urgency and importance, then prioritize accordingly.

```
            NOT URGENT         URGENT
IMPORTANT   Do soon            Do first
            Long-term goals    Deadlines coming up
            
NOT IMP.    Eliminate          Minimize
            Distractions       Reactive tasks
            Time-wasters       Interruptions
```

**How it works:**
- Make a list of everything you need to do
- For each item, ask: "Is this important? Is this urgent?"
- Categorize accordingly
- Focus on the Important/Urgent quadrant (do first)
- Then Important/Not Urgent (schedule it)
- Minimize Not Important/Urgent
- Eliminate Not Important/Not Urgent

**Pros:**
- Helps you see what really matters
- Prevents you from just reacting to urgency
- Works well with priorities
- Flexible and adaptable

**Cons:**
- Requires honest assessment
- "Important" is subjective
- Doesn't show you *how* to do things
- Can take time to set up

**Best for:** People with lots of competing demands, anyone struggling to prioritize

### 3. The Pomodoro Technique (Time + Breaks)

**What it is:** You work in focused 25-minute blocks with 5-minute breaks in between.

**How it works:**
1. Choose one task to work on
2. Set a timer for 25 minutes and work intently (no phone, no distractions)
3. When timer goes off, take a 5-minute break
4. Repeat 3-4 times, then take a longer break (15-30 minutes)

**Why it works:**
- 25 minutes is long enough to do real work, short enough to stay focused
- Breaks prevent burnout
- Knowing the break is coming helps you focus
- The timer creates urgency

**Pros:**
- Simple to implement
- Helps with procrastination
- Builds focus muscle
- Works for any task

**Cons:**
- Some tasks need longer unbroken time
- Breaks can be disruptive in group work
- Timer pressure doesn't work for everyone

**Best for:** Procrastinators, anyone who struggles with focus, tasks that feel overwhelming

### 4. The Two-List System

**What it is:** You keep a master list of everything you need to do, and a daily list of 3-5 priorities.

**How it works:**
- Master list: Everything you need to do (in a notebook or app)
- Daily list: Pick 3-5 things to accomplish today
- Do those things. Everything else waits.
- Tomorrow, pick 3-5 new priorities from the master list

**Pros:**
- Simple and flexible
- Prevents overwhelm (only focusing on 3-5 things)
- Easy to use
- Can combine with other systems

**Cons:**
- Requires discipline not to add more than 3-5
- Daily planning takes time
- Doesn't help with long-term scheduling

**Best for:** Flexible people, people who get overwhelmed, anyone starting out with time management

## Procrastination: Why You're Doing It, How to Stop

Real talk: **Procrastination is not a character flaw. It's not laziness.** 

Procrastination usually happens for one of these reasons:

**1. The task feels overwhelming**
- Solution: Break it into smaller steps. Start with the smallest step.

**2. You're not sure how to do it**
- Solution: Ask for help first. Then start with what you know.

**3. You're afraid of doing it badly**
- Solution: Remember Module 1—growth mindset. You're learning. It's okay if it's not perfect.

**4. You don't know why it matters**
- Solution: Connect it to your purpose. Why are you taking this class? Why will you need this skill?

**5. The task is genuinely not a priority**
- Solution: Say no. Or reschedule for when it actually matters.

**6. You're using it to self-soothe (escape)**
- Solution: You might be stressed or overwhelmed. Address the root cause. Take real breaks, not avoidance breaks.

### Anti-Procrastination Strategies

**1. Start tiny**
- Not "Write the essay."
- But "Spend 5 minutes outlining the essay."
- Once you start, momentum often builds.

**2. Make it visible**
- Write it on your calendar or to-do list
- Seeing it makes it real, not avoidable

**3. Use accountability**
- Tell someone your deadline
- Join a study group that will ask you about it
- Text a friend "I'm going to start this in 5 minutes"

**4. Change your environment**
- If home feels procrastination-prone, go to the library
- Sometimes a new place helps you focus

**5. Use the Pomodoro Technique**
- 25 minutes of real work is manageable
- You can do anything for 25 minutes

**6. Address the root feeling**
- If you're procrastinating because you're anxious, deal with the anxiety (breathing, talking to someone)
- If you're procrastinating because it's boring, find a way to make it more interesting

**7. Schedule it**
- Put it on your calendar
- Treat it like a class (you wouldn't skip that)

## Energy, Not Just Time

Here's something time management misses: **Not all hours are created equal.**

When you're tired, one hour of studying might feel like two. When you're energized, two hours might feel like one.

### Understanding Your Energy

- **When is your peak energy?** Morning? Afternoon? Late evening?
- **What activities drain you?** (Different for everyone)
- **What activities recharge you?** (Different for everyone)
- **How do sleep, food, and exercise affect your energy?**

**Pro move:** Schedule your hardest work when your energy is highest.

Don't save math homework for 11 PM if your brain is fried by then. Do it at 2 PM when you're sharp.

Don't schedule all of your draining activities in one day if you can spread them out.

## The Reality: You Can't Do It All

Here's the truth: **You cannot do everything perfectly.**

You probably can't:
- Get A's in every class
- Join 5 clubs
- Work 30 hours a week
- Maintain a perfect social life
- Exercise daily
- Sleep 8 hours
- Eat perfectly
- Have zero stress

Something's got to give. The question is: **What do you intentionally choose not to do?**

This is not failure. This is strategy. This is choosing what matters and letting go of what doesn't.

## Key Takeaways This Week
