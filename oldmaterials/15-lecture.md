# Module 15: Engaging in College Community

## Finding Your People: College Community & Belonging

Here's something nobody talks about until students are struggling: **Who you spend time with matters. A lot.**

Research on college outcomes shows something clear: **Students who find community, build relationships, and engage on campus persist in college at higher rates and report higher satisfaction.**

But it's not automatic. You have to find your people. You have to take the first step.

This week: **You engage with your college community intentionally.**

---

## Why Community Matters (The Research)

College is hard. Not just academically—but socially and emotionally.

You're:
- Making major life decisions
- Managing stress and anxiety
- Figuring out who you are
- Building identity and confidence

**Having people near you who get it—who are also figuring it out—changes everything.**

Research shows that students with strong campus connections:
- Have higher GPA
- Are more likely to graduate
- Report better mental health
- Experience greater belonging
- Have higher life satisfaction

**This isn't just "nice to have." It's foundational.**

---

## The Belonging Challenge

So why don't all students build community?

### The Barriers

**1. It's Hard to Start**
Especially for introverts, or if you transferred, or if you're coming from a different background. Walking into a club meeting and introducing yourself feels vulnerable. There's risk of rejection.

**2. Everyone Looks Like They Already Know People**
By sophomore year, friend groups are formed. It feels like you're late. (You're not, but it feels that way.)

**3. You're Busy**
Between classes, work, and studying, adding "go meet people" feels like one more thing.

**4. You Might Be Different**
First-gen college student. Commuter. Working 20+ hours. Different race or ethnicity. LGBTQ+. International. Older. In some campus spaces, you might feel out of place.

**5. Online/Hybrid/Flexible Format**
If you're taking classes online or have a non-traditional schedule, it's even harder. No built-in community gathering points.

**Here's the truth:** *All of these barriers are real. And most students feel them.*

So when you find community despite these barriers, that's real resilience.

## Types of Community on Campus

Community isn't just "make friends." It's finding places where you belong.

### Academic Community

- Study groups in your major
- Labs, studios, or practicum sessions
- Department clubs (pre-law, future teachers, biology club, art collective, etc.)
- Research projects or thesis groups
- Honors programs or learning communities

**The benefit:** These communities get what you're studying. They push you academically and support your goals.

### Interest-Based Community

- Hobby clubs (gaming, rock climbing, gardening, crafting, cooking)
- Arts (theater, music, visual arts, creative writing)
- Sports and recreation
- Outdoor or adventure groups

**The benefit:** You're doing something you love with people who love it too.

### Cultural & Identity Community

- Cultural organizations (Latin Student Association, Black Student Union, Asian Student Association, LGBTQ+ resource center, etc.)
- Faith-based communities
- First-gen student programs
- International student organizations
- Affinity groups

**The benefit:** People who share parts of your identity. This is powerful when you feel like an outsider.

### Service & Activism Community

- Community service organizations
- Environmental groups
- Social justice organizations
- Volunteer programs
- Fundraising clubs

**The benefit:** Working toward something bigger than yourself with people who share your values.

### Community for YOU

You might find your people in:
- Your residence hall
- Your work-study job
- A shared class
- A campus event
- Online (yes, this counts)
- A campus mentor or organization

## Finding YOUR Community

### Step 1: Inventory What Matters to You

Finish these sentences:
- "I love doing..."
- "I care about..."
- "I want to make a difference in..."
- "I've always wanted to try..."
- "When I'm with these types of people, I feel..."

### Step 2: Explore What's Available

Most colleges have:
- Club fair at the beginning of the year
- Online club directory
- Student activities office
- Flyers and bulletin boards around campus

**Go explore.** You don't have to join anything. Just see what exists.

### Step 3: Show Up (To One Thing)

Pick one club, organization, or community event and actually go.

Not: "I'll probably show up"
Actually go. In person. (Or online if that's how they meet.)

**What happens in that first meeting:**
- People are welcoming (because they want new members)
- You might feel awkward (normal)
- You might meet one person you click with (that's plenty for starting)
- You'll get a sense of whether this group is for you

### Step 4: Do It Again (If It Clicks)

After one meeting:
- Do you want to go back?
- Did you meet someone interesting?
- Does this align with your interests?

If yes: Go to the next meeting. Sit with someone you talked to. Say hi.

If no: Try another group. That's okay.

### Step 5: Go Deeper

Once you've found a group that fits:
- Go regularly (consistency builds relationships)
- Get involved (officer role, volunteer, event planning)
- Build friendships outside the context (grab food together, text, hang out)

## Overcoming Barriers to Belonging

### If You're an Introvert

- Start with smaller groups or one-on-one conversations (coffee with one person is easier than a big mixer)
- Choose communities where you can engage in your preferred way (create, work on projects, deep conversation—not necessarily "large party energy")
- Remember: Introversion doesn't mean you don't want community. You just get energy differently.

### If You're First-Generation College Student

- Connect with first-gen programs on campus (most colleges have them)
- Find mentor relationships (professors, staff, older students)
- Recognize your perspective is a strength, not something to hide
- Connect with peers also navigating college first

### If You're Commuting

- Choose one or two organizations to commit to (easier than spreading yourself thin)
- Find online communities (Discord, Slack groups for your major)
- Connect during the day rather than always evening events
- Reach out to other commuters (you're not alone)

### If You're Working

- Look for work-study or on-campus jobs (built-in community)
- Choose one organization and go consistently (even if just once a week)
- Find online communities or asynchronous engagement
- Connect with other working students

### If You're From a Different Background

- Seek communities where you see people like you
- Know that it takes time to find cultural belonging
- Connect with affinity groups or cultural organizations
- Find mentors who share your background
- Check: Are campus institutions genuinely welcoming and inclusive, or just performing?

## Building Real Relationships

Community isn't just showing up. It's building actual relationships.

### Move From "Acquaintance" to "Friend"

These are the stages:
1. **Meet someone** (club meeting, first day, random conversation)
2. **Talk a few times** (club events, hallway conversations)
3. **Do something outside that context** ("Want to grab coffee?" "Study together?" "Come over this weekend?")
4. **Text between hangouts** (memes, questions, life stuff)
5. **Know each other's lives** (you know their struggles, their dreams, their family situation)

Most college students stay at stage 2. Real friendship requires moving to stage 3.

**You have to take the step.** "Want to grab lunch?" is scary. People almost always say yes.

### Depth Over Breadth

You don't need a huge friend group. Most people can deeply maintain 3-5 close relationships.

Better: 3-4 close friends who really know you
Than: 20 acquaintances you see at events

Invest in depth.

### Be Vulnerable

Relationships deepen when you're real.

- Share what you're struggling with (not immediately, but eventually)
- Ask for help (this is hard; let someone support you)
- Show your authentic self (not the "perfect college student" version)
- Be interested in others' real lives, not just surface stuff

## Online Community Matters Too

If you're studying online, or just prefer it initially:

**Online communities count as community.** They can:
- Provide study support and academic belonging
- Offer emotional support and connection
- Build identity around shared interests
- Create meaningful relationships

Examples:
- Discord servers for your major or classes
- Reddit communities around your interests
- Gaming communities
- Online affinity groups
- Study group video calls

## Campus Involvement & Leadership

Once you're engaged, you might want to get more involved.

### Benefits of Leadership

Joining clubs is great. Getting involved in leadership (officer role, planning events, etc.) is powerful because:
- You build deeper relationships (working together on something)
- You develop skills (project management, decision-making, communication)
- You have impact (you shape the community)
- It's resume material (future employers value this)

### Getting Involved

- Ask club leadership if they need help
- Express interest in specific events or projects
- Show up consistently (reliability matters)
- Volunteer for smaller roles first (event planning, social media, small project)

## Discussion Prompts & Reflection

### Individual Reflection (Do This First)

**1. Your Belonging**
- Where do you feel you belong on campus right now?
- What communities have you found (or not)?
- What's made it easy or hard to find community for you?

**2. What Matters to You**
- What brings you joy?
- What do you care about?
- What kinds of people do you want to spend time with?
- Finish: "I'd love to find people who..."

**3. Your Barriers**
- What's kept you from engaging more?
- Is it time, confidence, fit, or something else?
- What would help?

**4. Your Next Step**
- What's one community you could explore?
- What's one event you'll attend?
- What's one person you'll invite to grab coffee?

### Online Discussion: Finding Your People

**Prompt 1: Your Community (So Far)**
Where have you found community on campus? What made that group feel like "home"?

**Prompt 2: What Brought You In**
What was your entry point? (A friend who invited you? A club fair? A class? An interest?)

**Prompt 3: The Ask**
What community are you looking for but haven't found? What would you want it to look like?

Respond to at least two peers. Share invitations. Share recommendations.

## Your Action Plan This Week (Week 15)

### Activity 1: Reflect on Belonging (30 min)

- Answer the individual reflection questions
- Think about what matters to you
- Identify what barriers you're experiencing

### Activity 2: Explore Campus Community (1 hour)

- Visit your college's student activities website or club directory
- Look for organizations aligned with your interests
- Write down 3-5 communities to explore

### Activity 3: Show Up to Something (2-3 hours)

This is the main event.

- Choose one club, organization, or community event
- Commit to attending
- Go
- Introduce yourself to at least one person
- Don't overthink it—just show up and be yourself

### Activity 4: Take the Next Step (varies)

After attending:
- Did it feel like your kind of community?
- If yes: Plan to go to the next meeting. Exchange contact info with someone if you click.
- If no: Choose a different community and try again.

### Activity 5: Reach Out (15 min)

- Message one person from campus
- "Hey, I'm in your [class/club/dorm]. Want to grab coffee this week?"
- Plant the seed for a hangout

### Activity 6: Engage in Discussion (1.5 hours)

- Do reflection prompts
- Post to forum
- Respond to at least 2 peers
- Encourage each other toward community

## Key Takeaways This Week

- **Belonging isn't automatic.** You have to find your people, take the first step, and keep showing up.
- **Your barriers are real, and they're shared.** Everyone's figuring out how to belong.
- **You can be yourself and still find community.** Authenticity beats perfection.
- **Depth matters more than breadth.** One real friendship is worth more than a hundred acquaintances.
- **Community starts with one conversation.** One invitation. One "show up."

## One More Thing

**Belonging is a basic human need.** We're wired to connect.

College is one of the best times in your life to build community. You're surrounded by people with similar challenges, shared interests, developmental stages. It's literally designed for connection.

But you have to take the step. Nobody will force you to belong. You have to do the vulnerable thing: show up, introduce yourself, say "I want to be part of this."

**That takes courage. You can do it.**

And once you find your people—the ones who get you, who challenge you, who celebrate with you—that's what college will be about. Not the classes (though those matter). But the people.
